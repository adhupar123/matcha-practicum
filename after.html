After

This section evaluates how matcha quality is understood across different cultural contexts. By comparing Japanese and global perspectives, quality emerges not as a fixed standard, but as something shaped by values, experience, and intention.

Through interviews, tastings, and observation, two distinct frameworks of evaluation became visible. One prioritizes tradition, restraint, and continuity, while the other often emphasizes accessibility, flavor intensity, and individual preference. This comparison does not aim to determine which is “correct,” but instead to understand how different systems produce different definitions of quality.

Japanese Perspective

Quality as Restraint

Quality is defined by balance, subtlety, and harmony rather than intensity. Bitterness is minimized, and umami is integrated rather than dominant.

Expand
High quality matcha is not meant to be overwhelming. Instead, it reflects control across the entire production process. This perspective became clearer through the tea ceremony, where even small imbalances felt disruptive.

Process Over Outcome

Attention is placed on cultivation methods, preparation, and ritual rather than just the final taste.

Expand
Shading duration, grinding technique, and preparation method are all considered part of quality. This reframes matcha as a process-driven product rather than a purely consumable one.

Cultural Context

Matcha is embedded within tradition, particularly the tea ceremony, which shapes how it is evaluated and experienced.

Expand
Quality extends beyond taste into behavior, environment, and intention. The act of drinking becomes inseparable from the cultural framework surrounding it.

Global / International Perspective

Quality as Intensity

Stronger flavor, vibrant color, and noticeable bitterness are often interpreted as indicators of quality.

Expand
Many consumers associate boldness with quality, similar to coffee culture. This contrasts with the subtle balance emphasized in traditional contexts.

Outcome Over Process

Focus is placed on the final drink rather than how it was produced or prepared.

Expand
Convenience products such as matcha lattes shift attention away from traditional preparation, making quality more about immediate sensory impact.

Adaptation and Fusion

Matcha is frequently incorporated into new formats such as desserts, flavored drinks, and wellness products.

Expand
This expands accessibility but also alters the original meaning of matcha. Through interviews, I noticed that flexibility is often valued more than authenticity in global contexts.
SYNTHESIS

Reframing Matcha Quality

Through this comparison, matcha quality no longer appears as a universal standard, but as something constructed through cultural values and context. In Japanese practice, quality is embedded in restraint, continuity, and ritual precision. In global contexts, it often becomes associated with intensity, accessibility, and personal preference.

Rather than viewing one perspective as superior, this project suggests that matcha exists across multiple systems of meaning. Its globalization does not simply dilute tradition, but transforms how quality is defined and experienced. This shift raises broader questions about how cultural products evolve when removed from their original context.

Ultimately, matcha quality is not just a property of the leaf, but a relationship between production, preparation, and perception.
