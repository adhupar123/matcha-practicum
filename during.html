Matcha Lifecycle System

This diagram maps the full agricultural and preparation system behind ceremonial-grade matcha — from cultivar selection and shading strategy to milling, storage, and final sensory expression. Hover over each point to explore how individual variables interact across the lifecycle.

Cultivar (品種) Genetic variation determines baseline sweetness, bitterness, and shade tolerance.

Cultivars such as Yabukita, Okumidori, and Samidori differ in amino acid concentration, leaf thickness, and aromatic expression. Genetic selection shapes the plant’s potential long before cultivation begins.
Shading Strategy (覆下栽培) Pre-harvest shading regulates biochemical composition.

Reducing sunlight for 3–4 weeks preserves L-theanine and chlorophyll while limiting catechin development. The method of shading — tana shelving versus direct covering — alters microclimate stress and flavor balance.
First Flush (一番茶) Harvest timing defines sweetness and fiber content.

Early spring leaves contain higher amino acid density and thinner cellular structure. Later harvests increase fiber and catechins, producing harsher texture and reduced vibrancy.
Steaming & Drying Post-harvest treatment prevents oxidation.

Leaves are steamed immediately after picking to halt enzymatic browning. Controlled drying preserves chlorophyll stability and aromatic integrity before stems and veins are removed to create tencha.
Stone Milling (石臼挽き) Mechanical refinement shapes texture and suspension.

Granite mills rotate slowly to avoid heat buildup, producing particles averaging 5–10 microns. This ultra-fine powder forms a stable colloidal suspension, creating ceremonial creaminess.
Storage & Oxidation Environmental control preserves sensory quality.

Exposure to oxygen, light, and humidity degrades volatile compounds and dulls chlorophyll vibrancy. Cold storage and airtight packaging maintain aromatic complexity.
Preparation Variables Water chemistry and whisking technique influence perception.

Soft water enhances sweetness and foam stability. Temperature control (70–80°C) prevents bitterness amplification. Whisk aeration alters texture and aromatic release.
Sensory Integration Color, aroma, umami, bitterness, and texture converge here.

Matcha quality is not determined by a single variable but by system-level balance. Cultivation, processing, and preparation variables interact to shape the final embodied experience.
