During

This phase follows the full lifecycle of matcha from cultivation to tea ceremony. Through direct engagement, each step reveals how biological processes, human intervention, and ritual practice collectively shape the perception of quality.

Cultivation

Planting and Soil Preparation

Biology Foundation

Matcha quality begins long before harvest. Soil composition and cultivar selection determine the plant’s biochemical potential, particularly its ability to produce amino acids like L-theanine that drive sweetness and umami.

View Details
Tea plants are grown in nitrogen-rich soil, which directly increases amino acid synthesis. Over time, careful pruning encourages lateral growth, producing softer, more desirable leaves. What initially felt like a distant agricultural detail became more meaningful as I began to connect these early decisions to the sensory differences I experienced in tasting.
Pre-Harvest

Shading the Plants

Chemistry Technique

Several weeks before harvest, tea plants are shaded to alter their chemical composition and enhance desirable flavor characteristics.

View Details
Shading reduces sunlight exposure, slowing photosynthesis and preventing L-theanine from converting into catechins. This preserves sweetness while reducing bitterness. Chlorophyll levels increase, resulting in a deeper green color. Seeing this process reframed matcha quality as something intentionally engineered rather than naturally occurring.
Harvest

First Flush Picking

Quality Observation

The first harvest yields the youngest, most chemically rich leaves, which are essential for high-grade matcha.

View Details
First flush leaves contain higher concentrations of amino acids and lower structural fiber. This results in smoother texture and reduced astringency. Experiencing different grades of matcha made it clear how dramatically harvest timing influences final quality.
Processing

Steaming

Chemistry Preservation

Fresh leaves are immediately steamed to halt oxidation and preserve their chemical structure.

View Details
Steaming deactivates enzymes responsible for oxidation, preserving chlorophyll and preventing browning. This step locks in the chemical qualities developed during cultivation. Understanding this helped explain why Japanese green tea maintains such a distinct flavor profile compared to oxidized teas.

Drying and Tencha Production

Processing

Leaves are dried and refined into tencha, the raw material used to produce matcha powder.

View Details
Unlike other teas, leaves are not rolled but laid flat to dry. Stems and veins are removed, leaving only soft leaf tissue. This reduces bitterness and prepares the material for ultra-fine grinding.
Final Production

Stone Grinding

Technique Application

Tencha is ground into a fine powder using traditional stone mills.

View Details
Grinding occurs slowly to prevent heat buildup, which can degrade amino acids and aromatic compounds. The resulting powder is extremely fine, allowing it to suspend evenly in water and create a smooth, creamy texture. This stage made it clear how delicate the transition from leaf to powder really is.
Preparation

Whisking and Water Control

Application Experiment

Preparation transforms matcha from a static material into an active sensory experience.

View Details
Water temperature and whisking technique significantly affect flavor and texture. Lower temperatures enhance sweetness, while improper whisking can lead to uneven suspension. Experimenting with these variables revealed how much control the preparer has over perceived quality.
Tea Ceremony

Ritual and Execution

Culture Observation

The tea ceremony reframes matcha as more than a beverage, emphasizing ritual, presence, and intentionality.

View Details
Before preparation, utensils are cleaned and arranged in a deliberate sequence. Tea is prepared with precise, practiced movements. Guests rotate the bowl before drinking and consume the tea immediately to preserve its qualities. Afterward, utensils are appreciated and the experience is acknowledged. This process revealed that matcha quality is deeply tied to context, not just chemistry.
Reflection

Redefining Matcha Quality

Evaluation Synthesis

Matcha quality emerged as a combination of biological potential, technical execution, and cultural context.

View Details
Rather than being a fixed property, quality became something constructed through interaction between plant, producer, preparer, and environment. This shifted my understanding from a purely technical framework to a more holistic and relational one.
