During

This phase traces the full lifecycle of matcha from cultivation to consumption. Through direct experience and observation, this timeline connects biological processes, production techniques, and cultural practices to evolving perceptions of quality.

Cultivation Foundations

Soil Preparation and Cultivar Selection

Biology Foundation

Matcha quality begins at the agricultural level. Soil composition rich in nitrogen supports amino acid synthesis, particularly L-theanine. Cultivar selection determines genetic potential for sweetness, bitterness, and color. Shade tolerant cultivars are specifically chosen for their ability to retain chlorophyll and amino acids under reduced light conditions.

Growth and Leaf Development

Biology Observation

As tea plants grow, photosynthesis regulates the balance between amino acids and catechins. Exposure to sunlight increases catechin production, contributing to bitterness. Observing this relationship reframed quality as something biologically dynamic rather than fixed.

Pre Harvest Shading

Shading Process (覆下栽培)

Technique Chemistry

Tea plants are shaded for several weeks before harvest, reducing photosynthesis. This prevents the conversion of L-theanine into catechins, preserving umami and sweetness. Chlorophyll production increases, deepening the vibrant green color associated with high grade matcha.

Harvest and Processing

First Flush Harvest (一番茶)

Quality Observation

The first harvest yields the youngest, most nutrient dense leaves. These leaves contain higher concentrations of amino acids and lower fiber content, resulting in smoother texture and reduced astringency. Experiencing high quality matcha highlighted how critical timing is to overall quality.

Steaming and Drying

Processing Chemistry

Leaves are immediately steamed after harvest to halt enzymatic oxidation. This preserves chlorophyll and prevents browning. Drying stabilizes the leaf for storage while maintaining volatile aroma compounds. These steps protect the chemical integrity established during cultivation.

Tencha Refinement

Processing

After drying, stems and veins are removed, leaving only soft leaf material known as tencha. This refinement reduces bitterness and improves texture, preparing the material for grinding into matcha.

Grinding and Preparation

Stone Grinding (石臼挽き)

Technique Application

Tencha is ground using granite stone mills at very low speeds to prevent heat buildup. This produces ultra fine particles around 5 to 10 microns, allowing matcha to suspend evenly in water and create a smooth mouthfeel.

Water and Whisking Variables

Application Experiment

Through hands on preparation, changes in water temperature and whisking technique significantly altered flavor and texture. Lower temperatures enhanced sweetness, while whisking influenced foam stability and perceived creaminess.

Tea Ceremony and Consumption

Tea Ceremony Experience

Culture Observation

The tea ceremony emphasized intentionality, precision, and presence. Quality extended beyond taste to include ritual, environment, and interaction. This introduced a cultural dimension that cannot be replicated through production knowledge alone.

Shift in Perception of Quality

Evaluation Synthesis

Quality became a balance between biological potential, technical execution, and experiential context. Rather than being a fixed property, matcha quality emerged as something constructed through interaction between material, preparation, and perception.
