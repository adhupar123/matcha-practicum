During

This section follows the complete lifecycle of matcha, from cultivation to tea ceremony. Each stage reveals how biological processes, human intervention, and ritual practice shape the final perception of quality.

Planting and Cultivation

Tea plants are cultivated from carefully selected cultivars in nitrogen rich soil to promote amino acid production.

View Details
Seeds or cuttings are planted in controlled soil environments. Nitrogen availability increases L-theanine synthesis, which contributes to sweetness and umami. Over several years, plants are pruned to encourage lateral growth, producing tender leaves ideal for matcha production.

Shading Process

Plants are covered for several weeks before harvest to manipulate chemical composition.

View Details
Shading reduces sunlight exposure, slowing photosynthesis. This prevents L-theanine from converting into catechins, preserving sweetness and reducing bitterness. Chlorophyll concentration increases, producing the deep green color associated with high quality matcha.

First Harvest

Young leaves are hand picked at peak biochemical quality.

View Details
The first flush (一番茶) produces leaves with the highest amino acid content and lowest fiber. Early harvesting ensures softer texture and smoother flavor. Delayed harvesting increases cellulose and bitterness.

Steaming

Leaves are rapidly steamed to preserve chemical integrity.

View Details
Steaming halts enzymatic oxidation by deactivating polyphenol oxidase. This prevents browning and preserves chlorophyll and aromatic compounds. This step differentiates Japanese green tea from oxidized teas like black tea.

Drying and Tencha Production

Leaves are dried and refined into tencha before grinding.

View Details
Leaves are air dried without rolling, maintaining flat structure. Stems and veins are removed to isolate soft leaf material. This reduces bitterness and prepares the material for fine grinding.

Stone Grinding

Tencha is ground into ultra fine powder using stone mills.

View Details
Granite mills rotate slowly to avoid heat buildup. Heat can degrade volatile aroma compounds and amino acids. The result is a fine powder (5–10 microns) that suspends evenly in water.

Preparation

Matcha is sifted, whisked, and dissolved into water.

View Details
Powder is sifted to remove clumps. Water temperature is typically 70–80°C to balance extraction. Bamboo whisking introduces air, creating foam and improving texture. Whisking technique directly affects mouthfeel.

Pre Ceremony Ritual

The tea ceremony begins with purification and intentional preparation.

View Details
Participants cleanse hands and mentally prepare. Utensils are carefully arranged and cleaned in front of guests. These actions emphasize mindfulness and respect, setting the tone for the experience.

Serving and Drinking

Tea is prepared and consumed with deliberate movements and etiquette.

View Details
The host prepares the tea with precise movements. Guests rotate the bowl before drinking as a sign of respect. Matcha is consumed immediately to preserve temperature and texture.

Post Ceremony Reflection

The ceremony concludes with appreciation and reflection.

View Details
Utensils are examined and appreciated. Guests acknowledge the host and the experience. The emphasis is on gratitude, transience, and presence, reinforcing cultural values embedded in the practice.
