Matcha Lifecycle System

This page separates the agricultural determinants of matcha from the production techniques and sensory outcomes. Together, these systems define ceremonial-grade quality.

Plant & Agricultural Determinants

Cultivar (品種) Genetic lineage determines biochemical ceiling.

Different cultivars vary in L-theanine synthesis, chlorophyll density, and catechin balance. Shade-tolerant cultivars such as Samidori exhibit deeper umami and smoother bitterness integration.
Soil Composition (土壌) Mineral density and nitrogen availability influence amino acid production.

Balanced microbial ecosystems enhance root nutrient absorption, affecting sweetness, structure, and long-term plant resilience.
Harvest Timing (一番茶) First flush leaves contain maximal L-theanine and minimal fiber.

Later harvests increase cellulose and catechin concentration, producing stronger bitterness and reduced vibrancy.
Shading Strategy (覆下栽培) Three to four weeks of shading alters photosynthetic metabolism.

Reduced sunlight preserves chlorophyll and slows conversion of L-theanine into catechins, intensifying umami and softening astringency.

Processing, Preparation & Sensory Expression

Processing (製茶工程) Steaming (蒸し) halts enzymatic oxidation. Drying (乾燥) stabilizes chlorophyll before stems and veins are removed to create tencha.
Stone Milling (石臼挽き) Granite mills grind slowly to prevent heat buildup.

Particles average 5–10 microns, enabling smooth suspension and velvety texture.
Freshness Management (鮮度管理) Oxygen, light, and humidity degrade volatile compounds.

Cold storage preserves chlorophyll vibrancy and aromatic clarity.
Water Character (湯質) Mineral composition and temperature (70–80°C) influence sweetness and foam structure.

Hard water amplifies bitterness; soft water enhances umami expression.
Color (色) Chlorophyll concentration determines vibrancy.

Deep jade coloration indicates effective shading and minimal oxidation.
Aroma (香り) Volatile aldehydes and grassy compounds define first perception.

Aromatic complexity diminishes rapidly with oxygen exposure.
Umami (旨味) Driven primarily by L-theanine concentration.

Higher amino acid density produces sweetness and depth without harshness.
Bitterness (渋み) Catechin concentration determines astringency.

Controlled shading and harvest timing moderate harsh phenolic expression.
Texture (質感) Micron-level particle size defines mouthfeel.

Ultra-fine powder forms a stable colloidal suspension, creating ceremonial creaminess.
