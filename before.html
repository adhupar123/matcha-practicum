Matcha Anatomy

This diagram maps the biological, chemical, and mechanical variables that define ceremonial-grade matcha. Hover over each trait to explore how cultivation technique translates into sensory experience.

Color (色) The vivid green hue reflects chlorophyll preservation during pre-harvest shading.
Shading tea plants for 3–4 weeks reduces photodegradation and slows the conversion of L-theanine into catechins. Elevated chlorophyll density signals amino acid retention, careful canopy management, and higher ceremonial classification.
Umami (旨味) Amino acid retention defines matcha’s sweetness and depth.
L-theanine accumulates under low-light conditions. This shifts the biochemical balance toward glutamate-driven savoriness, producing the soft, rounded profile associated with high-grade Uji matcha. Excess sunlight converts these amino acids into more bitter catechins.
Bitterness (渋み) Catechin concentration governs astringency and structure.
Increased UV exposure accelerates catechin biosynthesis. While catechins contribute antioxidant capacity and structural sharpness, excessive concentration disrupts balance and reduces smoothness, often indicating lower ceremonial quality.
Texture (質感) Micron-level particle refinement shapes mouthfeel.
Traditional granite stone mills grind tencha into particles averaging 5–10 microns. This ultra-fine powder forms a stable colloidal suspension in water, producing the creamy, velvety texture valued in tea ceremony practice.
Aroma (香り) Volatile compounds shape the first sensory impression.
Fresh milling preserves aldehydes and grassy volatiles responsible for marine and vegetal notes. Aromatic integrity declines rapidly with oxygen exposure and humidity, making storage conditions critical to perceived quality.
