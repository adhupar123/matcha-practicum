Matcha Lifecycle System

A systems-level map of ceremonial-grade matcha — integrating genetics, soil ecology, harvest timing, processing, storage, and preparation into a single agricultural continuum. Hover over each point to explore how individual variables interact to shape final sensory expression.

Cultivar (品種) Genetic lineage determines biochemical potential.

Varieties such as Yabukita, Okumidori, and Samidori differ in amino acid density, chlorophyll concentration, and shade tolerance. Genetic selection defines the ceiling of sweetness, aroma complexity, and bitterness balance before cultivation even begins.
Soil Composition (土壌) Mineral density and nitrogen availability shape leaf chemistry.

Balanced soil microbiomes influence amino acid synthesis and stress response. Regions such as Uji are prized for mineral-rich volcanic soils that contribute structural smoothness and depth.
Harvest Timing (一番茶) Early spring harvest maximizes sweetness.

First flush leaves contain higher L-theanine and lower fiber content. Later harvests increase catechins and cellulose, producing stronger bitterness and coarser texture.
Shading Strategy (覆下栽培) Controlled light exposure preserves chlorophyll and amino acids.

Three to four weeks of shading reduces photosynthetic stress, limiting catechin formation and intensifying umami concentration. Shading technique alters microclimate stress patterns.
Steaming & Drying Immediate steaming prevents enzymatic oxidation.

Heat treatment stabilizes chlorophyll and halts browning reactions. Leaves are dried and deveined to produce tencha before grinding.
Stone Milling (石臼挽き) Particle size determines mouthfeel.

Granite mills produce powder averaging 5–10 microns while minimizing heat. This ultra-fine structure creates the creamy suspension characteristic of ceremonial matcha.
Storage Stability Oxidation degrades color and aroma.

Exposure to oxygen, light, and humidity dulls chlorophyll vibrancy and diminishes volatile aromatic compounds. Refrigerated, airtight storage preserves complexity.
Water Quality Mineral content alters flavor perception.

Soft water enhances sweetness and foam stability, while hard water amplifies bitterness and suppresses aromatic clarity. Temperature control (70–80°C) prevents excessive astringency.
Sensory Integration All variables converge in final perception.

Matcha quality emerges from system-level balance. Genetics, soil, harvest timing, processing precision, storage integrity, and preparation technique collectively determine color, aroma, texture, and umami harmony.
