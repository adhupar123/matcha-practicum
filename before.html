Sensory Expression
Cultivar (品種)
  • Genetic lineage determines leaf morphology, chlorophyll density, and enzyme expression.
  • Shade-adapted cultivars upregulate L-theanine synthase, producing elevated L-theanine and balanced catechin levels.
  • Chlorophyll biosynthesis and flavonoid pathways modulate both color and bitterness.
  • Leaf development and cell wall composition affect infusion efficiency and texture.
Soil Composition (土壌)
  • Balanced minerals and optimal nitrogen enhance amino acid synthesis and photosynthetic efficiency.
  • Magnesium, calcium, and potassium regulate enzymatic pathways for chlorophyll and flavonoid production.
  • Beneficial microbes facilitate nitrogen fixation, indirectly boosting L-theanine.
  • Soil pH influences catechin accumulation, affecting bitterness and astringency.
Harvest Timing (一番茶)
  • First flush leaves deliver maximal L-theanine and minimal fiber due to reduced lignification.
  • Early harvesting preserves chlorophyll and amino acid content, enhancing smooth texture and subtle sweetness.
  • Delayed harvesting increases catechin synthesis, raising astringency and structural complexity.
  • Photosynthetic activity prior to harvest optimizes chlorophyll-to-carotenoid ratios.
Shading Strategy (覆下栽培)
  • Controlled shading downregulates catechin synthesis while upregulating L-theanine pathways.
  • Reduced UV exposure decreases polyphenol oxidase activity, preserving chlorophyll and enhancing umami.
  • Shading maintains lustrous jade color and improves sweetness-to-bitterness balance.
  • Photoreceptor signaling adjusts amino acid metabolism for richer flavor profiles.
Processing (製茶工程)
  • Immediate steaming deactivates polyphenol oxidase, halting catechin oxidation.
  • Rolling and drying maintain cell wall integrity, influencing amino acid and volatile compound release.
  • Proper moisture management preserves L-theanine stability, maintaining refined flavor balance.
  • Controlled oxidation prevents bitter note development while maintaining color and sweetness.
Stone Milling (石臼挽き)
  • Slow granite milling prevents frictional heat, preserving volatile aldehydes, pyrazines, and chlorophyll derivatives.
  • Ultra-fine particles increase infusion efficiency and creamy mouthfeel.
  • Milling rate influences catechin release, controlling perceived astringency and bitterness.
  • Particle size affects colloidal suspension stability, contributing to ceremonial texture.
Freshness Management (鮮度管理)
  • Cold storage slows enzymatic oxidation and Maillard reactions.
  • Low oxygen and controlled humidity preserve L-theanine, chlorophyll, and aroma compounds.
  • Storage stability ensures consistent infusion, color, and flavor intensity.
  • Prevents leaf degradation, maintaining clarity of sweetness and vibrant appearance.
Water Character (湯質)
  • Mineral content and pH influence extraction of L-theanine, caffeine, and catechins.
  • Soft water enhances sweetness and umami perception; hard water accentuates catechin bitterness.
  • Water composition modulates solubility of aroma compounds, impacting bouquet and mouthfeel.
  • Temperature and mineral balance affect suspension stability and infusion texture.
Color (色)
  • Leaf and powder color reflects chlorophyll integrity preserved by shading, steaming, and milling.
  • High chlorophyll a/b ratios and minimal pheophytin formation maintain vivid jade hues.
  • Minor carotenoids contribute yellow-green undertones, signaling freshness and careful processing.
  • Color intensity correlates with amino acid and flavonoid balance, indicating quality.
Aroma (香り)
  • Steaming preserves green aldehydes (hexanal, cis-3-hexenal) for fresh grassy notes.
  • Stone milling liberates pyrazines and terpenoids, adding roasted and marine nuances.
  • Mild drying promotes subtle Maillard volatiles without creating bitterness.
  • Volatile compounds interact with water to enhance bouquet perception in the cup.
Umami (旨味)
  • L-theanine synergizes with glutamic acid, activating T1R1/T1R3 receptors for deep, lingering umami.
  • Shading and rapid steaming maximize amino acid concentration.
  • Minor nucleotides (IMP, GMP) enhance taste complexity and depth.
  • Balanced infusion temperature preserves umami without activating excessive bitterness.
Bitterness (渋み)
  • Catechin composition (EGCG, epicatechin) defines structural tension and flavor complexity.
  • Steaming and storage limit oxidation, balancing bitter and sweet notes.
  • Protein-polyphenol interactions during infusion modulate astringency and mouthfeel.
  • Infusion time and water temperature influence perceived bitterness intensity.
Texture (質感)
  • Ultra-fine powder ensures homogeneous colloidal suspension for smooth infusion.
  • Polysaccharides and lipids enhance viscosity, creating ceremonial creaminess.
  • Proper whisking preserves microbubbles, producing luxurious mouthfeel.
  • Particle size distribution affects sedimentation rate and mouthfeel stability.
Three Layers of Quality
Layer 01 · Biology
The leaf before it is touched
Cultivar genetics, soil composition, shading strategy, and harvest timing form the biological foundation. Everything that follows can only preserve or diminish what begins here.
Layer 02 · Craft
Precision across every step
Steaming, rolling, stone milling, and storage each introduce or close off possibilities. The craftsperson's choices translate biological potential into something the cup can hold.
Layer 03 · Sensory
Where the system becomes experience
Color, aroma, umami, bitterness, and texture are not isolated qualities — they are the accumulated expression of every prior decision. The cup is the diagram made visible.
