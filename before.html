Matcha Anatomy

Hover over each point to explore how cultivation technique translates into sensory and chemical quality.

Color (色) The vivid green hue reflects chlorophyll preservation during pre-harvest shading.

Shading tea plants for 3–4 weeks reduces photodegradation and slows the conversion of L-theanine into catechins. Elevated chlorophyll density signals amino acid retention, careful canopy management, and higher ceremonial classification.
Umami (旨味) Amino acid retention defines matcha’s sweetness and depth.

L-theanine accumulates under low-light conditions, shifting biochemical balance toward glutamate-driven savoriness. This produces the soft, rounded profile associated with high-grade Uji matcha.
Bitterness (渋み) Catechin concentration governs astringency and structural sharpness.

Increased UV exposure accelerates catechin biosynthesis. While essential for antioxidant capacity, excessive concentration disrupts balance and reduces smoothness.
Texture (質感) Micron-level particle refinement shapes mouthfeel.

Traditional granite stone mills grind tencha into particles averaging 5–10 microns, forming a stable colloidal suspension that produces the creamy texture valued in tea ceremony.
Aroma (香り) Volatile compounds shape the first sensory impression.

Fresh milling preserves grassy aldehydes and marine notes. Aromatic integrity declines rapidly with oxygen and humidity exposure.
